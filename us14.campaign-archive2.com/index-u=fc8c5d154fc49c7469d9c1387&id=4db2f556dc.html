Community Day - Monday 17 October

Halcyon students will be out and about around London for most of Monday 17 October on a variety of exciting excursions. The day will start at school, so please arrive as normal at 0900. Students will be released from school as normal - at 1600 - unless otherwise noted. After school activities will take place as usual.

Grade 6 British Museum
Grade 6 are visiting the British Museum to explore the use of masks used by different cultures.  This will give the students an opportunity to look at the way the various cultures have used materials, textures and patterns to express and transform the performer.  Students are working with masks both in drama and art and will be able to continue their research, making connections for their ideas to enable them to begin constructing their plaster masks over the coming weeks.

Students need to bring:

  • a fully topped up Oyster card
  • a fully charged iPad
  • money for lunch or a packed lunch
  • comfortable shoes and a jacket
  • a full water bottle
Students should meet in the art room (2.10) 9.00 am Monday. Please contact Sue Dance if you have any questions.

Grade 7 Queen Elizabeth Olympic Park Trip

Students will acquire an appreciation and respect for various cultures through skills developed in PE (orienteering) and mathematics (measuring). Students will also learn about the health benefits of parks and open places for local communities.

Spanish debate about "Columbus Day"
On Thursday, our Spanish students held a debate examining several celebrations that occur on 12 October in different countries around the world.  The discussion focused on the controversy around the festivity celebrated in the USA as Columbus Day, in Spain as 'Día de la Hispanidad' and National Day and in most of the countries in Latin America  as 'Día de la Raza' , 'Día de la resistencia indígena', or 'Día de la Hispanidad'. On the one hand this festivity represents the idea of the union of different cultures under the identity of the same language; on the other hand, it is contended that this date coincides with the genocide of indigenous populations such the Inca, Aztec or Maya.

Halcyon students attend LSE lecture on the Bangladeshi Liberation War
On Thursday 13 October, some Grade 10 students and a parent accompanied Ms Cooke and Ms Gonzalez to a talk by Dr Nayanika Mookherjee on the legacy of rape during the Bangladeshi Liberation War of 1971. Other academics in related fields contributed to the discussion. Themes discussed included the narrative of the Birangona - the labelling of the victims of rape as 'war heroines'; how the events changed relations between men and women in Bangladesh and the role of ethnicity, religion and gender in the conflict and its aftermath.  Furthermore, the difficulties of hearing from the multitude of different voices with different experiences and balancing that with prosecutions and the slow pace of the justice system. Students are currently starting to prepare for a conference on sexual violence in conflict using different languages of expression and this talk certainly gave them an important insight into the challenges facing justice and legacy in the aftermath of war crimes.



Mark Levy, life-long Civil Rights activist, speaks at Halcyon today
Many students as well as some staff and a parent enjoyed hearing Facing History and Ourselves speaker, Mark Levy, an American life-long Civil Rights activist speak about his life and specifically his experiences as part of the Civil Rights Movement during the late 1950’s and 1960’s. Mark was part of the Freedom Schools which were connected to the Freedom Riders Movement. Spending the summer with his wife in the home of a black family in Mississippi was a life-threatening situation for all of them due to the intense racism of the time and the local, active Ku Klux Klan. The Freedom Schools were formed by trained volunteers who spent time training the next generation of activists in the South of the US in any facility made available to them, from front porches and fields to derelict seminaries. The curriculum Mark and others were trained to deliver focused on helping young blacks examine and clarify what this integrated world they were working toward would look like. What did white society have that they would like to have? What did black culture have that they wanted to see in this new world? What things did neither have that they could work toward together? A thoughtful and unifying approach in very tough times. Students were able to make connections to some of today’s issues both locally and internationally with regard to identity, belonging and oppressive behaviours.
